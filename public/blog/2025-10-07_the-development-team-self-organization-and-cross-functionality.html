The Development Team: Self-Organization and Cross-Functionality

Introduction

Imagine a surgical team where the lead surgeon assigns every single task: “You make this incision. You hold this tool. You monitor that vital sign.” The surgery would be slow, inefficient, and dangerous.

Now imagine that same team working in sync, each member knowing their role, anticipating needs, collaborating seamlessly, making real-time adjustments without waiting for instructions. That’s the power of self-organization.

In Scrum, the Development Team isn’t a group of individuals taking orders. They’re a cohesive unit that organizes their own work, makes their own technical decisions, and holds themselves accountable for delivering value.

This is radically different from traditional software development, where managers assign tasks, architects dictate solutions, and developers simply execute. Scrum trusts the people closest to the work to make the best decisions about how to do it.

Let me show you what makes Development Teams in Scrum unique, powerful, and incredibly effective when done right.

What is a Development Team?

In Scrum, “Developers” includes everyone who contributes to creating the Increment:

  • Software engineers
  • Testers/QA engineers
  • Database administrators
  • UI/UX designers
  • Technical writers
  • DevOps engineers
  • Security specialists
  • Any other specialized role needed

Key principle: There are no sub-teams or hierarchies within Developers. No “frontend team” vs “backend team.” No “senior” vs “junior” formal distinctions. Everyone is simply a Developer.

Team Size: The Magic Number

Scrum Guide recommendation: 3 to 9 Developers (not counting Product Owner and Scrum Master)

Why fewer than 3 doesn’t work:

  • Insufficient skill diversity
  • Limited perspective
  • High risk if someone is unavailable
  • Difficult to maintain momentum

Why more than 9 doesn’t work:

  • Communication overhead grows exponentially
  • Coordination becomes complex
  • Daily Scrum becomes unwieldy
  • Accountability diffuses

The sweet spot: 5–7 Developers for most teams

The Two Core Characteristics

Scrum Development Teams are defined by two fundamental characteristics that make them exceptional.

Characteristic 1: Self-Organization

What Self-Organization Means

Self-organization means the team decides internally how to accomplish their work. Nobody outside the team assigns tasks, dictates solutions, or micromanages execution.

What they decide:

  • Who works on which item
  • How to approach technical problems
  • What tools and technologies to use
  • How to organize their daily work
  • When to ask for help
  • How to improve their process

What they don’t decide:

  • What to build (that’s the Product Owner)
  • When the Sprint ends (that’s the timebox)
  • The Sprint Goal (collaborative decision in Sprint Planning)

Why Self-Organization Works

People Closest to Work Know Best Developers understand technical complexity better than managers. They can make better decisions about implementation.

Ownership Creates Accountability When people choose their work, they own the outcomes. When assigned, they’re just following orders.

Engagement Increases Autonomy is one of the strongest motivators. People who control their work are more engaged and productive.

Better Solutions Emerge Diverse perspectives collaborating produce better solutions than single authorities dictating.

Faster Adaptation Self-organizing teams adapt to changing circumstances in real-time without waiting for management approval.

What Self-Organization Looks Like

Sprint Planning: The Product Owner presents the Sprint Goal and Product Backlog items. Developers collaborate to determine what they can accomplish and create a plan. Nobody assigns tasks — they’re pulled based on skills, interest, and Sprint Goal needs.

During the Sprint: Developer A finishes a story. They don’t wait for assignment. They look at the Sprint Backlog, consider the Sprint Goal, and pull the next highest-priority item they’re capable of working on.

When Problems Arise: The database server goes down. Developers don’t escalate and wait. They assess: Can we work around it? Should we call IT? Can we use local environments? They decide and act.

Daily Scrum: Developers run the meeting themselves. They discuss progress, identify blockers, and make plans. No manager runs the meeting or assigns tasks based on updates.

Real-World Example: Self-Organization in Action

The Situation: Mid-Sprint, a critical bug is discovered in production. It’s not in the Sprint Backlog, but it’s affecting users.

Traditional Command-and-Control Approach: Manager sees the bug. Assigns Developer X to drop everything and fix it. Developer X was working on a critical Sprint Backlog item. That item stalls. Sprint Goal is at risk. Developer X has limited context on the bug and struggles.

Self-Organized Approach: The team discusses the bug in Daily Scrum. Developer Y has deep knowledge of that code area and volunteers to investigate. Developer Z offers to pair with Y because they have availability. Developer A adjusts their plans to cover Y’s original work. The team decides: fix the bug, then assess if Sprint Goal is still achievable or if scope needs adjustment. They inform the Product Owner of the decision.

The Difference: The traditional approach creates dependency and delays. The self-organized approach leverages collective intelligence and adapts in real-time.

Stages of Self-Organization

Level 1: Task-Focused Team members pick tasks but work independently. Limited collaboration. Still somewhat siloed.

Level 2: Collaborative Team members help each other. They pair program, review code, share knowledge. Collaboration emerges.

Level 3: Adaptive Team anticipates problems, reorganizes work dynamically, makes real-time adjustments without discussion. They operate like a jazz ensemble — improvising together while staying in sync.

Level 4: Optimizing Team continuously experiments with new practices, challenges assumptions, teaches other teams. They’re constantly improving.

Most teams start at Level 1 and progress over multiple Sprints. Patience and trust are essential.

Common Self-Organization Challenges

Challenge 1: “Nobody Takes the Hard Tasks”

Why it happens: Team members gravitate toward comfortable, familiar work. Difficult tasks sit untouched.

Solution:

  • Make work visible on the board
  • Discuss in Daily Scrum: “This task hasn’t moved in 3 days. What’s blocking it?”
  • Pair experienced with less experienced developers on hard tasks
  • Celebrate learning and growth, not just completion
  • Team creates agreements: “We won’t start new work while red items sit”

Challenge 2: “Some People Do All the Work”

Why it happens: A few high-performers carry the load. Others coast.

Solution:

  • Make work distribution visible
  • Discuss in Retrospectives: “Is work being shared equitably?”
  • Rotate different types of work
  • Address individual performance issues directly but respectfully
  • Ensure Definition of Done includes review, so no work is truly “solo”

Challenge 3: “Decisions Take Forever”

Why it happens: Team tries to reach consensus on everything. Discussions drag on.

Solution:

  • Establish decision-making frameworks: “We discuss for 15 minutes, then decide”
  • Use “Disagree and Commit” — share perspectives, make a decision, everyone commits
  • Empower individuals for appropriate decisions: “If you’re doing the work, you decide the approach”
  • Reserve team decisions for things that truly affect everyone

Challenge 4: “We Keep Waiting for Permission”

Why it happens: Team is used to command-and-control. They wait for approval even when self-organized.

Solution:

  • Scrum Master coaches: “What’s stopping you from making this decision?”
  • Establish clear boundaries: “You have authority over technical decisions”
  • Celebrate when team makes autonomous decisions
  • Leadership explicitly gives permission to self-organize

Characteristic 2: Cross-Functionality

What Cross-Functionality Means

A cross-functional team has all the skills necessary to deliver a Done Increment without depending on people outside the team.

This doesn’t mean:

  • Everyone knows everything
  • No specialization exists
  • Everyone does every type of work

This does mean:

  • Collectively, the team has all needed skills
  • Team can deliver complete features from start to finish
  • No handoffs to other teams required for standard work
  • Team isn’t blocked waiting for external dependencies

Why Cross-Functionality Matters

Speed No waiting for other teams or departments. Work flows from start to completion within one team.

Quality Different perspectives (design, development, testing) are integrated from the beginning, not bolted on at the end.

Reduced Handoffs Each handoff risks miscommunication and delay. Cross-functional teams minimize handoffs.

Shared Understanding When everyone participates in all phases, understanding is deeper and more consistent.

Flexibility If one skill area has less work, that person can contribute elsewhere instead of being idle.

What Cross-Functionality Looks Like

A complete cross-functional team might include:

  • Frontend developers (React, Angular, etc.)
  • Backend developers (APIs, services, databases)
  • QA/Test engineers
  • UX/UI designers
  • DevOps/infrastructure specialists
  • Security experts (at least knowledge)
  • Business analysts (or Product Owner fulfills this)

How they work together:

Feature: User Password Reset

Traditional Siloed Approach:

  1. BA writes requirements (3 days)
  2. Designer creates mockups (2 days, waiting for BA)
  3. Frontend developer waits for designs (starts 5 days in)
  4. Backend developer works in parallel but lacks full context
  5. Integration happens late, issues discovered
  6. QA tests at the end, finds bugs
  7. Back to developers for fixes
  8. Total: 3–4 weeks

Cross-Functional Approach:

  1. Whole team discusses user need in Refinement
  2. Designer sketches ideas, developers provide technical input
  3. Frontend and backend developers collaborate on API design
  4. All work in same Sprint, coordinating daily
  5. QA writes tests alongside development, not after
  6. Team demos working feature together
  7. Total: 1 Sprint (2 weeks)

Building Cross-Functionality

Strategy 1: T-Shaped People

Encourage team members to be “T-shaped”:

  • Deep expertise in one area (the vertical part of T)
  • Broad knowledge across multiple areas (the horizontal part of T)

Example: A backend specialist who can also do basic frontend work and understand UX principles.

How to develop T-shaped skills:

  • Pair programming across specialties
  • Rotate work occasionally
  • Lunch-and-learns where specialists teach others
  • Code reviews that educate, not just critique

Strategy 2: Collective Code Ownership

No single person “owns” specific parts of the codebase. Anyone can work on any part.

Benefits:

  • Knowledge spreads across the team
  • No bottlenecks waiting for “the expert”
  • More eyes on code means better quality
  • Bus factor increases (team isn’t helpless if someone leaves)

How to implement:

  • Pair programming rotations
  • Mandatory code reviews
  • Documentation of complex areas
  • “No heroes” culture — share knowledge, don’t hoard it

Strategy 3: Swarming

When a high-priority item needs completion, multiple team members swarm on it rather than working in parallel on different items.

Benefits:

  • Features complete faster
  • Quality improves through collaboration
  • Knowledge spreads naturally
  • Blockers get resolved immediately

When to swarm:

  • Critical items blocking the Sprint Goal
  • Complex work that benefits from multiple perspectives
  • When the team is behind and needs to focus

Strategy 4: Minimal Viable Specialization

Have enough specialization to be effective, but not so much that people can’t help outside their specialty.

Poor balance: “I only do React. I never touch the backend.”

Good balance: “I specialize in React, but I can help with backend API issues when needed.”

Real-World Example: Cross-Functionality in Action

The Situation: A team is building a mobile app feature that requires frontend UI, backend API, database changes, and integration testing.

Non-Cross-Functional Team:

  • 3 frontend developers, 2 backend developers, 1 QA engineer
  • Frontend finishes first, waits for backend
  • Backend discovers frontend assumptions were wrong, needs changes
  • QA starts testing after everything is “done,” finds integration bugs
  • Ping-pong of fixes between frontend and backend
  • Feature takes 3 Sprints

Cross-Functional Team:

  • Team has 5 developers with mixed skills, 1 QA engineer
  • Entire team discusses feature in Refinement
  • Frontend and backend developers pair on API design
  • QA writes acceptance tests before development starts
  • Developers work simultaneously, integrating continuously
  • Daily Scrum catches misalignments immediately
  • Feature completes in 1 Sprint

The Difference: Cross-functionality eliminates handoffs and enables parallel, coordinated work.

How Self-Organization and Cross-Functionality Work Together

These two characteristics are complementary and mutually reinforcing.

Self-Organization enables Cross-Functionality: When teams organize their own work, they naturally collaborate across specialties because it’s more effective than waiting for assignments.

Cross-Functionality enables Self-Organization: When teams have all needed skills, they can make autonomous decisions without external dependencies.

Together, they create:

  • Speed: Fast delivery without handoffs or waiting
  • Quality: Built-in quality from multiple perspectives
  • Adaptability: Real-time response to changes
  • Innovation: Creative solutions from diverse collaboration
  • Engagement: Motivated people solving problems together

Practices That Support High-Performing Development Teams

Practice 1: Pair Programming

What it is: Two developers work on the same code simultaneously — one “driving” (typing), one “navigating” (reviewing, suggesting).

Benefits:

  • Immediate code review
  • Knowledge transfer
  • Fewer bugs
  • Better designs through collaboration
  • Less isolation

When to use:

  • Complex problems
  • Onboarding new team members
  • Working in unfamiliar code areas
  • When stuck on a problem

Practice 2: Mob Programming

What it is: The whole team works on the same thing, at the same time, in the same space (physical or virtual).

Benefits:

  • Extreme knowledge sharing
  • Fastest learning
  • Highest quality (many eyes)
  • Great for complex problems

When to use:

  • Extremely complex features
  • Critical production bugs
  • Learning new technologies
  • Establishing coding standards

Practice 3: Code Reviews

What it is: Peer review of code before it’s merged, ensuring quality and sharing knowledge.

Best practices:

  • Review promptly (within hours, not days)
  • Focus on learning, not criticism
  • Checklist of quality standards
  • Everyone reviews, everyone gets reviewed

Practice 4: Definition of Done

What it is: A shared, explicit checklist of quality standards that all work must meet.

Example Definition of Done:

  • Code written and peer-reviewed
  • Unit tests written (>80% coverage)
  • Integration tests passing
  • No critical security vulnerabilities
  • Code meets style standards
  • Documentation updated
  • Accepted by Product Owner

Why it matters: Creates shared understanding of “complete” and prevents technical debt.

Practice 5: Continuous Integration

What it is: Developers integrate code into the main branch frequently (daily or multiple times daily), with automated testing.

Benefits:

  • Integration issues discovered immediately
  • Always have working software
  • Reduces merge conflicts
  • Enables continuous delivery

Practice 6: Team Norms and Working Agreements

What it is: Explicit agreements about how the team works together.

Example agreements:

  • “Core hours are 10 AM — 3 PM when everyone is available”
  • “We rotate pairing partners every two days”
  • “No code merges without review”
  • “We respond to messages within 2 hours”
  • “Friday afternoons are for learning time”

How to create: Discuss in Retrospectives. Write them down. Review periodically.

The Developer’s Responsibilities

Individual developers on Scrum teams have specific accountabilities:

Creating the Sprint Plan

Not just accepting a plan, but creating it.

In Sprint Planning, developers forecast what they can complete and break down Product Backlog items into a detailed plan.

Adapting the Plan Daily

Responding to reality, not rigidly following the plan.

Daily Scrum is where developers inspect progress and adapt their plan for the next 24 hours.

Holding Each Other Accountable

Peer accountability, not manager oversight.

When a developer doesn’t meet commitments or quality standards, their teammates address it — respectfully but directly.

Continuous Improvement

Not just doing the work, but improving how they work.

Every Retrospective, developers identify improvements and implement them.

Quality Advocacy

Not compromising quality to meet deadlines.

Developers have the courage to say, “This doesn’t meet our Definition of Done. We need more time” rather than shipping technical debt.

Building Trust: The Foundation of High-Performing Teams

Self-organization and cross-functionality only work in high-trust environments.

What Trust Looks Like

Vulnerability-Based Trust Team members admit mistakes, ask for help, and show weakness without fear of judgment.

Example: “I don’t understand this technology. Can someone teach me?”

Reliability People do what they say they’ll do. Commitments are honored.

Competence Trust Team members believe in each other’s skills and judgment.

Example: “Sarah’s handling the database design. I trust she’ll make good decisions.”

Psychological Safety People feel safe to take risks, share ideas, and disagree without fear of punishment.

Building Trust

Consistency Show up. Do your work. Honor commitments. Trust builds through reliability.

Vulnerability Leaders and senior members must model vulnerability first. Admit mistakes openly.

No Blame When failures happen, focus on system improvement, not individual blame.

Celebrate Together Success is shared. Nobody is the hero. The team succeeds together.

Address Issues Promptly Don’t let resentments fester. Address problems respectfully when they’re small.

Common Anti-Patterns to Avoid

Anti-Pattern 1: The Hero Developer

What it looks like: One person does the majority of work, saves failing Sprints, and is treated as indispensable.

Why it’s harmful:

  • Creates single point of failure
  • Prevents team growth
  • Reduces collaboration
  • Builds dependency

How to fix:

  • Rotate work deliberately
  • Encourage heroes to mentor, not rescue
  • Celebrate team achievements, not individual heroics
  • Swarm on problems instead of relying on one person

Anti-Pattern 2: Siloed Specialization

What it looks like: “That’s frontend work. Only frontend developers touch it.”

Why it’s harmful:

  • Creates bottlenecks
  • Prevents learning
  • Reduces flexibility
  • Increases handoffs

How to fix:

  • Encourage T-shaped skills
  • Pair specialists with non-specialists
  • Rotate work types
  • Value learning over short-term efficiency

Anti-Pattern 3: Fake Self-Organization

What it looks like: Scrum Master or Product Owner assigns tasks, but calls it “self-organization.”

Why it’s harmful:

  • Destroys ownership
  • Reduces engagement
  • Prevents true collaboration
  • Team doesn’t develop self-organization skills

How to fix:

  • Leaders step back consciously
  • Ask “How will you approach this?” instead of “Here’s what you’ll do”
  • Let teams make mistakes and learn
  • Coach the team to organize, don’t organize for them

Anti-Pattern 4: The Absent Team

What it looks like: Team members work in isolation. Minimal collaboration. Daily Scrum is just status updates.

Why it’s harmful:

  • No collective ownership
  • Slow problem-solving
  • Reduced quality
  • Team is just a group, not a team

How to fix:

  • Emphasize Sprint Goal over individual tasks
  • Encourage swarming
  • Make collaboration visible and valuable
  • Create opportunities for pairing

Anti-Pattern 5: No Accountability

What it looks like: Team members don’t follow through on commitments. Nobody holds anyone accountable.

Why it’s harmful:

  • Predictability disappears
  • Trust erodes
  • Performance suffers
  • Sprint Goals aren’t achieved

How to fix:

  • Make commitments visible
  • Retrospectives address patterns
  • Peer feedback becomes normal
  • Product Owner and Scrum Master coach accountability

Measuring Team Health and Performance

Velocity

What it is: Story points completed per Sprint.

What it tells you:

  • Predictability over time
  • Impact of improvements
  • Capacity for planning

What it doesn’t tell you:

  • Actual value delivered
  • Quality of work
  • Team happiness

Use it for: Planning and tracking trends, not comparing teams.

Sprint Goal Success Rate

What it is: Percentage of Sprints where the Sprint Goal is achieved.

Why it matters: Better indicator of value delivery than velocity.

Team Happiness

What it is: Regular surveys or voting on team morale and satisfaction.

Example question: “On a scale of 1–5, how satisfied are you with team collaboration this Sprint?”

Why it matters: Happy teams are productive teams. Unhappiness is an early warning signal.

Quality Metrics

Examples:

  • Bug count
  • Production incidents
  • Technical debt
  • Code coverage
  • Security vulnerabilities

Why they matter: Speed without quality creates disaster. Monitor both.

Flow Efficiency

What it is: Percentage of time work is actively being worked on vs. waiting.

Example: Story takes 10 days total. 3 days of active work, 7 days waiting. Flow efficiency = 30%.

Why it matters: Reveals bottlenecks and waste in the process.

The Development Team Journey

Stage 1: Forming (Sprints 1–3)

Characteristics:

  • Politeness, testing boundaries
  • Unclear roles and norms
  • Dependence on Scrum Master and Product Owner
  • Low productivity

What helps:

  • Explicit working agreements
  • Clear Definition of Done
  • Patient coaching
  • Small, achievable Sprint Goals

Stage 2: Storming (Sprints 4–8)

Characteristics:

  • Conflicts emerge
  • Frustration with process or people
  • Competing for influence
  • Productivity varies

What helps:

  • Address conflicts directly but respectfully
  • Retrospectives focus on team dynamics
  • Scrum Master facilitates difficult conversations
  • Trust-building activities

Stage 3: Norming (Sprints 9–15)

Characteristics:

  • Working agreements solidify
  • Collaboration improves
  • Roles clarify
  • Productivity stabilizes

What helps:

  • Celebrate progress
  • Gradually reduce Scrum Master involvement
  • Encourage experimentation
  • Share successes with stakeholders

Stage 4: Performing (Sprints 16+)

Characteristics:

  • High trust and collaboration
  • Self-organization is natural
  • Continuous improvement is habit
  • Consistent high performance

What helps:

  • Challenge the team with stretch goals
  • Have them mentor other teams
  • Give them increasing autonomy
  • Recognize their achievements

Most teams reach Performing after 4–6 months. Some never do. It requires commitment, coaching, and time.

Conclusion

Development Teams in Scrum are fundamentally different from traditional software teams. Self-organization gives them autonomy and ownership. Cross-functionality gives them capability and speed.

Together, these characteristics create teams that:

  • Deliver value faster
  • Adapt to change immediately
  • Build quality into work
  • Continuously improve
  • Engage deeply in their work

But this doesn’t happen automatically. It requires:

  • Leadership that trusts and empowers
  • Time to build skills and relationships
  • Safe environments to make mistakes
  • Clear boundaries and support
  • Patience through the learning curve

The payoff is enormous. Self-organizing, cross-functional teams consistently outperform command-and-control structures. They innovate more, deliver faster, and create better products.

The question isn’t whether to build such teams, it’s whether you’re willing to create the conditions for them to thrive.
