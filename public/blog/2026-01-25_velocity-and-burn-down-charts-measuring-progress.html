Velocity and Burn-down Charts: Measuring Progress

Introduction

Imagine driving cross-country with no speedometer, no odometer, and no map. You’re moving, but you have no idea how fast, how far you’ve come, or how much farther you have to go. Are you on track? Should you adjust? When will you arrive? Pure guesswork.

This is how many teams operate — busy, working hard, but with no meaningful metrics to understand progress, forecast completion, or improve their process.

Velocity and burn-down charts solve this problem. They’re simple measurements that answer critical questions: How much work does this team typically complete? Are we on track this Sprint? When will we finish the backlog? Should we adjust our plan?

When used well, these metrics provide transparency, enable forecasting, and guide continuous improvement. When misused, they become weapons for blame, targets for gaming, and sources of dysfunction.

Let me show you how to measure progress effectively without falling into common metric traps.

What is Velocity?

Velocity is the amount of work a team completes in a Sprint, measured in story points.

Simple formula: Velocity = Story points completed in a Sprint

Example: Sprint 15: Team completes 5 user stories totaling 23 story points. Sprint 15 velocity: 23 points

Why Velocity Matters

Forecasting: Predict when work will be completed.

Sprint Planning: Determine how much work to pull into a Sprint.

Continuous Improvement: Track whether process improvements increase throughput.

Stakeholder Communication: Provide realistic timelines based on empirical data.

Self-Awareness: Understand team capacity and constraints.

What Velocity is NOT

Not a target to maximize: Velocity is a measurement, not a goal. Pushing for higher velocity leads to point inflation and quality shortcuts.

Not comparable across teams: Team A’s 30 points ≠ Team B’s 30 points. Points are team-specific.

Not a performance metric: Don’t use velocity to judge team members. It measures throughput, not individual performance.

Not stable immediately: New teams have erratic velocity for 3–5 Sprints. It stabilizes over time.

Not the only metric that matters: High velocity with low quality or wrong features is worthless.

Calculating Velocity

The Right Way

Count only completed work:

Sprint 16 plan:

  • Story A: 5 points ✓ Done
  • Story B: 8 points ✓ Done
  • Story C: 5 points ✓ Done
  • Story D: 8 points ✗ Not finished (moved to next Sprint)

Sprint 16 velocity: 5 + 8 + 5 = 18 points

Don’t count Story D. Partial credit creates false velocity.

The Wrong Ways

Wrong 1: Counting partial work “Story D is 80% done, so we’ll count 6.4 points.”

Problem: Not “Done” means not done. No partial credit.

Wrong 2: Adjusting completed points “Story B was harder than expected. Let’s change it from 8 to 13 points.”

Problem: Distorts historical data. The learning is that your estimates were optimistic — don’t hide that.

Wrong 3: Including stretch goals “We committed to 18 points but also completed 5 more points of bonus work.”

Problem: If you regularly complete “bonus” work, your commitment is too conservative. Adjust planning.

Tracking Velocity Over Time

Track velocity for at least 5–8 Sprints before using it for forecasting.

Example Velocity History:

Sprint Points Completed Notes 1 12 Learning, new team 2 15 Still finding rhythm 3 22 First good Sprint 4 18 One person on vacation 5 21 Normal capacity 6 23 Normal capacity 7 20 Normal capacity 8 22 Normal capacity

Average velocity (Sprints 4–8): 20.8 points

Use this for forecasting: Plan ~21 points per Sprint going forward.

Using Velocity for Forecasting

Forecasting Sprint Capacity

Question: How much work should we commit to in Sprint Planning?

Answer: Approximately your average velocity.

Example:

  • Average velocity: 24 points
  • Sprint 16 capacity: 100% (no vacations, no known interruptions)
  • Plan for: ~24 points of work

With adjustments:

  • Average velocity: 24 points
  • Sprint 17 capacity: 80% (one developer on vacation)
  • Plan for: ~19 points (24 × 0.8)

Forecasting Release Dates

Question: When will we complete the Product Backlog?

Example:

  • Product Backlog: 120 points remaining
  • Average velocity: 24 points per Sprint
  • Sprint length: 2 weeks

Calculation: 120 ÷ 24 = 5 Sprints = 10 weeks

Communication: “Based on current velocity, we’ll complete this work in approximately 5 Sprints, or about 10 weeks.”

Add buffer for uncertainty: “We forecast 5–6 Sprints to complete this work.”

Forecasting with Velocity Ranges

Velocity varies Sprint to Sprint. Account for this.

Example: Recent 5 Sprints: 20, 24, 18, 23, 21 points

Statistics:

  • Average: 21.2 points
  • Range: 18–24 points
  • Standard deviation: ~2.3 points

Forecast with range:

  • Optimistic (high velocity): 120 ÷ 24 = 5 Sprints
  • Realistic (average): 120 ÷ 21 = 6 Sprints
  • Conservative (low velocity): 120 ÷ 18 = 7 Sprints

Communication: “We forecast completing this work in 5–7 Sprints, most likely 6.”

What is a Burn-down Chart?

A burn-down chart shows remaining work over time, typically within a Sprint.

Y-axis: Work remaining (story points or tasks) X-axis: Time (days in Sprint)

Purpose: Visualize progress and predict Sprint outcome.

What Burn-down Charts Tell You

Scenario 1: On Track Actual line tracks close to ideal line. Interpretation: Team will likely complete Sprint commitment.

Scenario 2: Behind Schedule Actual line is above ideal line. Interpretation: Team is completing work slower than needed. Risk of not finishing. Action: Reduce scope, swarm on blockers, or accept partial completion.

Scenario 3: Ahead of Schedule Actual line is below ideal line. Interpretation: Team is completing work faster than planned. Action: Consider pulling in additional work from Product Backlog (if Sprint Goal allows).

Scenario 4: Flat Line No progress for multiple days. Interpretation: Team is blocked or working on unplanned items. Action: Daily Scrum should surface blockers. Scrum Master should investigate.

Scenario 5: Line Goes Up Remaining work increases mid-Sprint. Interpretation: Scope was added or work was underestimated. Action: Discuss in Daily Scrum. Negotiate with Product Owner if Sprint Goal is at risk.

Creating Effective Burn-down Charts

Daily Updates

Update the burn-down chart daily (typically during or after Daily Scrum).

Process:

  1. Team reviews Sprint Backlog
  2. Count remaining story points (or tasks)
  3. Plot new data point on chart
  4. Observe trend

This takes 2–3 minutes.

Physical vs. Digital

Physical Chart:

  • Large visible chart on team wall
  • Hand-drawn line added daily
  • High visibility
  • Tactile engagement
  • Great for co-located teams

Digital Chart:

  • Automatic from tools like Jira
  • Always up-to-date (if stories are updated)
  • Accessible remotely
  • Historical tracking
  • Great for distributed teams

Both work. Choose based on your context.

What to Burn Down

Option 1: Story Points Most common. Aligns with velocity.

Option 2: Task Hours More granular. Shows daily progress better.

Option 3: Number of Stories Simplest. Works if stories are similar size.

Recommendation: Start with story points. It aligns with your estimation and velocity metrics.

Burn-up Charts: The Alternative

Burn-down shows remaining work (decreasing). Burn-up shows completed work (increasing).

Why Burn-up is Sometimes Better

Advantage 1: Scope Changes are Visible

Burn-down problem: If scope increases mid-Sprint, the chart shows less progress (line goes up), which looks bad even if the team is productive.

Burn-up solution: Shows two lines:

  • Total scope (can increase)
  • Completed work (always increases)

Scope changes are explicit, not hidden in a confusing burn-down spike.

Advantage 2: Positive Framing

Burn-down: “We still have 12 points left” (focus on remaining) Burn-up: “We’ve completed 12 points” (focus on achievement)

Psychologically, some teams prefer the positive framing.

Release Burn-down Insights

Chart shows:

  • Are we on track for the release date?
  • Is velocity consistent?
  • Is scope creeping?
  • Do we need to adjust scope or timeline?

Scenario: Velocity drops If team consistently completes less than projected, release date will slip. Action: Reduce scope, add Sprints, or add team members (carefully — slows initial velocity).

Scenario: Scope increases If backlog grows faster than team completes work, release date extends indefinitely. Action: Product Owner must freeze scope or prioritize ruthlessly.

Common Metric Pitfalls

Pitfall 1: Using Velocity as a Performance Target

The Problem: “Team velocity was 30 last Sprint. This Sprint it must be 35!”

Why it fails:

  • Teams inflate estimates (every story becomes 13 points)
  • Quality drops (cut corners to finish more)
  • Trust erodes (metrics become games)

The Fix: Velocity is for forecasting, not evaluation. Track velocity, don’t target it.

Pitfall 2: Comparing Team Velocities

The Problem: “Team A does 40 points per Sprint. Team B only does 25. Team B is underperforming!”

Why it fails:

  • Points are team-specific (Team A’s “5” ≠ Team B’s “5”)
  • Creates unhealthy competition
  • Misses the point of relative estimation

The Fix: Each team’s velocity is meaningful only to that team. Never compare across teams.

Pitfall 3: Obsessing Over Daily Burn-down Fluctuations

The Problem: “Day 3 and we haven’t burned down any points! Panic!”

Why it fails:

  • Work doesn’t complete linearly
  • Early Sprint is often about starting work
  • Late Sprint is when completion happens
  • Daily variance is normal

The Fix: Look at trends over days, not single data points. Only worry if flat line persists for 3+ days.

Pitfall 4: Ignoring Context

The Problem: “Velocity dropped from 25 to 15. What’s wrong with the team?”

Missing context: One person was on vacation, and another was at a conference.

Why it fails: Velocity varies naturally based on capacity, complexity, and circumstances.

The Fix: Always consider context. Note capacity changes. Adjust expectations accordingly.

Pitfall 5: Burn-down Chart Theater

The Problem: Team spends 30 minutes daily meticulously updating the burn-down chart.

Why it fails: The chart is a tool, not the goal. Updating it should take minutes, not dominate Daily Scrum.

The Fix: Quick updates. Focus on the work, not the chart.

Pitfall 6: Changing Completed Story Points

The Problem: “That 5-point story actually took 8 points of effort. Let’s retroactively change it to 8.”

Why it fails:

  • Distorts historical velocity
  • Hides estimation inaccuracy
  • Prevents learning

The Fix: Keep original estimates. Track actual separately if needed. The variance is valuable data about estimation accuracy.

Actionable Insights from Metrics

Insight 1: Velocity is Declining

Observation: Last 3 Sprints: 24, 21, 18 points

Possible causes:

  • Technical debt accumulating
  • Team morale declining
  • Increasing complexity
  • More interruptions
  • Definition of Done strengthened

Actions:

  • Retrospective: Why is velocity dropping?
  • Invest in technical debt reduction
  • Address team impediments
  • Track interruption sources

Insight 2: Velocity is Highly Variable

Observation: Last 5 Sprints: 15, 28, 12, 25, 14 points

Possible causes:

  • Inconsistent estimation
  • Variable team capacity
  • Unpredictable scope changes
  • Poor Sprint Planning

Actions:

  • Improve estimation calibration
  • Account for capacity in planning
  • Protect Sprint from mid-Sprint changes
  • Refine backlog more thoroughly

Insight 3: Burn-down Shows Late Sprint Completion

Observation: No points burn down until Days 7–10 of Sprint

Possible causes:

  • Team works on easy tasks first, saves hard ones
  • Dependencies block early work
  • Work isn’t truly “In Progress” (status lag)
  • Large stories not broken down

Actions:

  • Swarm on high-priority items early
  • Identify dependencies in Sprint Planning
  • Update Sprint Backlog in real-time
  • Break down stories into smaller tasks

Insight 4: Burn-down Line Goes Up

Observation: Mid-Sprint, remaining work increases

Possible causes:

  • Scope added mid-Sprint (bad)
  • Work underestimated, tasks added (normal)
  • Team discovered necessary work (normal)

Actions:

  • If scope added: Discuss Sprint Goal impact with Product Owner
  • If tasks added: Normal. Continue.
  • If consistently underestimating: Improve estimation

Insight 5: Completing Less Than Planned Every Sprint

Observation: Team commits to 25 points, completes 18–20 consistently

Possible causes:

  • Chronic overcommitment
  • Interruptions not accounted for
  • Estimation too optimistic

Actions:

  • Plan for lower velocity (18–20, not 25)
  • Track and account for interruptions
  • Improve estimation accuracy
  • Be honest about capacity

Advanced Metrics

Predictability Metric

Measure: % of Sprint commitments completed

Calculation:

  • Sprint 1: Committed 25, completed 20 = 80%
  • Sprint 2: Committed 24, completed 24 = 100%
  • Sprint 3: Committed 23, completed 23 = 100%
  • Sprint 4: Committed 26, completed 21 = 81%

Average: 90% predictability

Target: >80% predictability over time

Use: Shows planning accuracy and stakeholder trust.

Cycle Time

Measure: Time from starting a story to completing it

Example: Story A: Started Day 2, completed Day 5 = 3 days cycle time

Track average cycle time: Lower is better (faster delivery)

Use: Identifies bottlenecks and flow issues.

Throughput

Measure: Number of stories completed per Sprint (regardless of size)

Example: Sprint 16: 6 stories completed Sprint 17: 8 stories completed

Use: Simple count. Good if stories are similar sized.

Defect Rate

Measure: Bugs found after “Done” / Total stories completed

Example: 10 stories completed, 2 had bugs found later = 20% defect rate

Target: Declining over time

Use: Quality indicator. High rate suggests weak Definition of Done.

Velocity and Burn-down Best Practices

Practice 1: Make Charts Visible

Physical teams: Large charts on wall where team works Remote teams: Dashboard everyone checks daily

Visibility creates accountability and transparency.

Practice 2: Update Daily

Burn-down charts are only useful if current.

Update during or immediately after Daily Scrum. Takes 2–3 minutes.

Practice 3: Discuss Trends, Not Points

In Daily Scrum: ❌ “We have 12 points remaining” ✓ “We’re trending below the ideal line. Should we pull in more work?”

Focus on trajectory, not absolute numbers.

Practice 4: Track Velocity for Learning, Not Judgment

Retrospective questions:

  • What affected our velocity this Sprint?
  • How accurate were our estimates?
  • What can we learn from velocity trends?

Not: Who didn’t pull their weight?

Practice 5: Use Multiple Metrics

Don’t rely on velocity alone:

  • Velocity (throughput)
  • Sprint Goal achievement (value delivery)
  • Quality metrics (defects, technical debt)
  • Team happiness (morale)

Together, these paint a complete picture.

Practice 6: Explain Metrics to Stakeholders

Help stakeholders understand:

  • What velocity means (and doesn’t mean)
  • Why it varies Sprint to Sprint
  • How it enables forecasting
  • Why comparing teams is invalid

Educated stakeholders use metrics appropriately.

Practice 7: Stop Tracking Unhelpful Metrics

If a metric isn’t driving decisions or learning, stop tracking it.

Metrics have costs:

  • Time to collect
  • Psychological impact
  • Risk of gaming

Only track what provides value.

Conclusion

Velocity and burn-down charts are simple yet powerful tools that bring transparency, enable forecasting, and guide continuous improvement.

Velocity tells you:

  • How much work your team typically completes
  • What to commit to in Sprint Planning
  • When you’ll complete the backlog
  • Whether process changes affect throughput

Burn-down charts show you:

  • Real-time Sprint progress
  • Whether you’re on track for Sprint Goal
  • When to adapt the plan
  • Patterns in how work completes

But remember:

  • Metrics are for learning and forecasting, not judgment
  • Velocity is team-specific, never compare across teams
  • Charts guide decisions, they don’t make them
  • Context always matters
  • Gaming metrics is easy; building trust and focusing on value is hard but worthwhile

Use these metrics wisely:

  • Track consistently
  • Analyze trends, not single data points
  • Combine with qualitative insights
  • Focus on continuous improvement
  • Maintain transparency
  • Avoid gaming and manipulation

When velocity and burn-down charts are used as intended — as tools for empirical process control and forecasting — they enable teams to plan confidently, deliver predictably, and improve continuously.

The goal isn’t perfect metrics. The goal is better decisions based on empirical data rather than hope and guesswork.
