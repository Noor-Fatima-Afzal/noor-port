Measures of Central Tendency: Mean, Median, Mode, Weighted Mean, and Trimmed Mean

Measures of central tendency are statistical metrics that describe the center point or typical value of a dataset. They provide a summary of the data, helping to understand and compare different datasets. In this article, we will explore the most common measures of central tendency: mean, median, mode, weighted mean, and trimmed mean. Each measure will be explained with its formula and practical examples.

Mean

The mean, also known as the average, is the sum of all the data points divided by the number of data points. It is a widely used measure of central tendency, particularly in situations where data points are evenly distributed.

Median

The median is the middle value of a dataset when the data points are arranged in ascending order. If the number of data points is odd, the median is the middle number. If the number of data points is even, the median is the average of the two middle numbers.

To find the median:

  1. Arrange the data points in ascending order.

Mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value in a dataset. A dataset can have one mode, more than one mode, or no mode at all if no value repeats.

Formula

There is no specific formula for the mode. It is determined by identifying the value that appears most frequently in the dataset.

Consider the dataset: 4, 2, 4, 6, 8, 4, 10.

The mode is 4, as it appears more frequently than any other value.

Trimmed Mean

The trimmed mean is the mean calculated after removing a specified percentage of the smallest and largest data points. This measure is useful to reduce the effect of outliers on the mean.

Formula

To find the trimmed mean:

  1. Arrange the data points in ascending order.
  2. Remove a specified percentage of the smallest and largest data points.
  3. Calculate the mean of the remaining data points.

Conclusion

Measures of central tendency provide a summary of the dataset’s central point, helping to understand and interpret data. The mean, median, and mode are the most common measures, each with its own advantages and appropriate use cases. The weighted mean is useful when certain data points have different levels of importance, and the trimmed mean is valuable for reducing the impact of outliers. By understanding these measures, you can effectively analyze and present data, enhancing your insights and decision-making in machine learning and other statistical applications.
