Applying Physical Constraints in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs): Methods and Examples

As machine learning (ML) continues to advance, its intersection with physical sciences has given rise to Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs). These hybrid models combine data-driven learning with physical knowledge such as differential equations, conservation laws, or empirical relationships. One of the most critical aspects of PINNs is the way they embed physical constraints into the learning process.

In this article, we explore various methods to enforce physical laws in PINNs, along with examples from wind power forecasting and other domains.

Why Physical Constraints Matter

Traditional neural networks can overfit, generalize poorly, or produce non-physical predictions (e.g., negative wind power output or sudden spikes in fluid velocity). Integrating physics helps:

  • Improve generalization with limited data.
  • Ensure physically plausible outputs.
  • Accelerate convergence by reducing the solution space.

Methods for Embedding Physical Constraints in PINNs

2. Architectural Constraints (Hard Constraints)

Instead of adding penalties, some physical laws can be embedded directly into the network structure to make them always satisfied.

Example:
 In fluid dynamics, a divergence-free condition for incompressible flow can be enforced by designing the output velocity field to satisfy this constraint automatically via custom activation functions or divergence-free layers.

In wind energy, you can design the final layer so outputs never exceed the turbine capacity or remain within physical power limits.

3. Physics-Informed Feature Engineering

Sometimes, it’s beneficial to inject physical knowledge by crafting physics-based input features.

Example:
 Instead of feeding raw wind speed, include derived variables like:

  • Kinetic energy ∝ v^2
  • Turbulence intensity
  • Wind shear or vertical gradients

These features encode known physical relationships and help the model learn faster and better.

4. Regularization via Physics (Soft Priors)

Add regularization terms that reflect physical intuition, not necessarily hard constraints. This allows flexibility while discouraging physically implausible outputs.

Example:
 Wind turbine power should generally increase monotonically with wind speed (within cut-in and rated speeds). Add a term that penalizes non-monotonic behavior in predicted power curves.

5. Post-Processing Corrections

In some cases, you can apply a physics-based correction after model prediction to align results with physical reality.

6. Hybrid Physical–ML Models

These models combine physical simulations (e.g., numerical weather prediction) with neural networks that learn from residual errors or fine-tune outputs.

Example:
 Use a numerical model to predict baseline wind power and a neural network to predict and correct the bias or residual error.

This allows leveraging strengths of both approaches — physical interpretability and data-driven flexibility.

Conclusion

Physics-Informed Neural Networks are a powerful tool for tackling complex forecasting problems in wind energy and beyond. While using the loss function to enforce physical constraints is common, a variety of methods can be employed depending on the nature of the problem, the physical laws involved, and the available data.

Understanding and strategically combining these methods allows us to build robust, interpretable, and generalizable models that bridge the gap between theory and data — a cornerstone for advancing renewable energy forecasting.
