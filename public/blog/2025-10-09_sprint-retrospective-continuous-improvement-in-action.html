Sprint Retrospective: Continuous Improvement in Action

Introduction

Imagine running a marathon but never looking at your pace, never adjusting your breathing, never learning from your mistakes. You’d run the same inefficient way every time, never improving, eventually burning out or giving up.

Athletes review every performance. Musicians practice difficult passages. Surgeons study complications. High performers in every field have one thing in common: they reflect, learn, and improve.

The Sprint Retrospective is Scrum’s built-in mechanism for continuous improvement. It’s where the team holds up a mirror, examines how they work, and commits to getting better. Not once a year in a performance review. Not when things are falling apart. Every single Sprint.

This is where magic happens. Where teams transform from groups of individuals into high-performing units. Where dysfunction becomes excellence through honest conversation and deliberate change.

Done well, Retrospectives energize teams and drive real improvement. Done poorly, they become complaint sessions that change nothing.

Let me show you how to run Retrospectives that teams look forward to and that genuinely transform how you work.

What is Sprint Retrospective?

The Sprint Retrospective is an opportunity for the Scrum Team to inspect itself and create a plan for improvements to be enacted during the next Sprint.

Duration: Maximum 3 hours for a one-month Sprint

  • For two-week Sprints: typically 1.5 hours
  • For one-week Sprints: typically 45 minutes

Participants: Scrum Team only (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers)

  • No external stakeholders
  • Safe space for honest conversation

Purpose:

  • Inspect how the last Sprint went regarding people, relationships, process, and tools
  • Identify what went well and what didn’t
  • Create a plan for implementing improvements
  • Adapt the Definition of Done if needed

When it happens: After Sprint Review, before next Sprint Planning

Output:

  • At least one actionable improvement for the next Sprint
  • Identified celebrations and successes
  • Updated team agreements or Definition of Done (if needed)
  • Renewed commitment and energy

What Retrospective is NOT

NOT a Blame Session Focus is on the system and process, not individual faults.

NOT Just Complaining Identifying problems without solutions is venting, not retrospecting.

NOT Optional Even “perfect” Sprints have room for improvement. Always hold Retrospective.

NOT About the Product Sprint Review inspects the product. Retrospective inspects the process.

NOT a One-Time Fix Improvement is continuous, not a single event.

NOT Open to External Stakeholders The team needs safety to speak honestly without external judgment.

Why Retrospective Matters

Without Retrospectives:

  • Teams repeat the same mistakes
  • Dysfunction becomes normalized
  • Small problems become big problems
  • Morale declines gradually
  • Improvement happens randomly, if at all
  • Teams plateau and stagnate

With Effective Retrospectives:

  • Continuous improvement becomes habit
  • Problems surface and get solved early
  • Team relationships strengthen
  • Processes evolve and optimize
  • Morale improves through empowerment
  • Team performance compounds over time

The Compounding Effect: Improving 1% every Sprint doesn’t sound dramatic. But after 25 Sprints (one year), you’re 28% better. After 50 Sprints, you’re 64% better. Small, consistent improvements compound into transformation.

The Core Principle: Prime Directive

Before every Retrospective, read or display the Prime Directive:

“Regardless of what we discover, we understand and truly believe that everyone did the best job they could, given what they knew at the time, their skills and abilities, the resources available, and the situation at hand.”

Why this matters:

  • Creates psychological safety
  • Prevents blame and defensiveness
  • Focuses on learning, not punishment
  • Assumes positive intent
  • Enables honest conversation

Without the Prime Directive (or similar framing), Retrospectives become accusatory. With it, they become productive.

The Retrospective Flow

Standard Five-Stage Structure

Most Retrospectives follow this proven pattern:

Stage 1: Set the Stage (5–10 minutes)

Purpose: Create the right environment and mindset.

Activities:

  • Welcome everyone
  • State the Prime Directive
  • Review the Sprint Goal and key events
  • Check-in activity to get everyone speaking

Example check-ins:

  • “In one word, how was this Sprint for you?”
  • “Rate the Sprint 1–5 and share why”
  • “What was your high and low point?”

Why this matters: People who speak early are more likely to participate throughout. Initial contribution lowers the barrier for continued participation.

Stage 2: Gather Data (15–20 minutes)

Purpose: Create shared understanding of what happened.

Activities:

  • Timeline the Sprint
  • Identify significant events
  • Surface different perspectives
  • Collect facts before opinions

Common techniques:

Timeline Exercise: Draw a timeline of the Sprint. Team members add:

  • Events (deployments, meetings, decisions)
  • Emotions (high points, low points)
  • Learnings (surprises, discoveries)

Mad/Sad/Glad: Team members write stickies in three categories:

  • Mad: What frustrated you
  • Sad: What disappointed you
  • Glad: What pleased you

Sprint Health Check: Rate different aspects 1–5:

  • Communication
  • Quality
  • Collaboration
  • Tools
  • Process

Why this matters: Shared data creates shared reality. Without common understanding, discussions are unproductive.

Stage 3: Generate Insights (20–30 minutes)

Purpose: Understand why things happened and identify patterns.

Activities:

  • Group similar items
  • Identify patterns and themes
  • Discuss root causes
  • Build shared understanding

Techniques:

Five Whys: For significant issues, ask “why” five times to reach root cause.

Example:

  • Problem: Build failed in production
  • Why? Tests didn’t catch the bug
  • Why? We don’t have integration tests
  • Why? No time to write them
  • Why? We’re always rushing to finish Sprint commitments
  • Why? We overcommit in Sprint Planning
  • Root cause: Need to improve Sprint Planning forecasting

Affinity Grouping:

  • Cluster similar items together
  • Name the clusters
  • Discuss patterns

Force Field Analysis:

  • What’s driving us toward our goal?
  • What’s restraining us?
  • How do we strengthen drivers and weaken restraints?

Why this matters: Without understanding root causes, you treat symptoms not diseases. Problems recur.

Stage 4: Decide What to Do (20–30 minutes)

Purpose: Identify concrete improvements and commit to implementing them.

Activities:

  • Generate possible improvements
  • Prioritize using voting or discussion
  • Select 1–3 improvements for next Sprint
  • Make improvements specific and actionable
  • Assign ownership

Key principles:

Be Specific:

  • Poor: “Improve communication”
  • Good: “Hold 15-minute sync meeting every Tuesday and Thursday at 10 AM”

Start Small: Better to succeed at one small change than fail at five big ones.

Assign Ownership:

  • Who will drive this improvement?
  • How will we track it?
  • When will we check progress?

Make It Measurable: How will we know if this worked?

Common prioritization methods:

Dot Voting: Each person gets 3 dots to vote on which improvements matter most.

Impact/Effort Matrix: Plot improvements on 2x2 grid:

  • High Impact, Low Effort = Do first
  • High Impact, High Effort = Plan carefully
  • Low Impact, Low Effort = Maybe
  • Low Impact, High Effort = Skip

Example improvements:

Instead of: “Better code quality” Try: “Pair program on all new features for the next Sprint”

Instead of: “Improve testing” Try: “Add one integration test per user story”

Instead of: “Communicate more” Try: “Product Owner to attend Daily Scrum three times per week”

Stage 5: Close the Retrospective (5–10 minutes)

Purpose: End on a positive note with clear commitments.

Activities:

  • Review selected improvements
  • Confirm who’s responsible for what
  • Appreciate what went well
  • Check-out activity

Example check-outs:

  • “What are you taking away from today?”
  • “How confident are you we’ll implement these improvements (1–5)?”
  • “One word to describe how you’re feeling now”

Appreciations: End by appreciating teammates:

  • “I appreciate Developer A for helping me when I was stuck”
  • “Thanks to the Product Owner for being so available this Sprint”

Why this matters: Ending positively maintains energy and optimism for the next Sprint.

Retrospective Formats and Activities

The standard format works, but variety prevents staleness. Here are proven alternatives:

Format 1: Start/Stop/Continue

Simple and effective for any team.

Three categories:

  • Start: What should we begin doing?
  • Stop: What should we cease doing?
  • Continue: What’s working that we should keep?

Process:

  1. Silent brainstorming (5 min)
  2. Share and group (10 min)
  3. Discuss and prioritize (20 min)
  4. Select improvements (10 min)

When to use: New teams, when energy is low, when you need simplicity.

Format 2: Sailboat/Speedboat

Visual and engaging metaphor.

Draw a boat with:

  • Wind (in sails): What’s propelling us forward?
  • Anchors: What’s holding us back?
  • Rocks ahead: What risks do we see?
  • Island (goal): Where are we going?

Process:

  1. Draw boat on whiteboard/virtual board
  2. Team adds items to each area
  3. Discuss patterns
  4. Identify improvements to add wind and remove anchors

When to use: Teams that like visuals, when energy is stagnant.

Format 3: 4Ls (Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For)

Comprehensive and balanced.

Four quadrants:

  • Liked: What did we enjoy/appreciate?
  • Learned: What did we discover?
  • Lacked: What was missing?
  • Longed For: What do we wish we had?

Process:

  1. Silent writing (5 min)
  2. Share and cluster (15 min)
  3. Discuss key themes (20 min)
  4. Generate improvements (15 min)

When to use: Mature teams, when you want deeper reflection.

Format 4: Starfish

More nuanced than Start/Stop/Continue.

Five categories:

  • Keep Doing: What’s working well
  • Less Of: What to reduce
  • More Of: What to increase
  • Stop Doing: What to eliminate
  • Start Doing: What to begin

When to use: When binary start/stop feels too rigid.

Format 5: Celebration Grid

Focus on learning from all outcomes.

Grid with four quadrants:

  • Practices: Expected success
  • Experiments: Unexpected success
  • Mistakes: Expected failure
  • Bad Luck: Unexpected failure

Focus: Learn from everything, celebrate experiments regardless of outcome.

When to use: Innovation-focused teams, when you want to encourage experimentation.

Format 6: Team Radar

Assess multiple dimensions.

Create radar chart with spokes:

  • Communication
  • Technical practices
  • Product Ownership
  • Team collaboration
  • Tools and environment
  • Process adherence

Rate each 1–5, plot on radar, discuss.

When to use: Quarterly deep-dives, when you want comprehensive assessment.

Advanced Retrospective Techniques

Technique 1: Silent Writing

Instead of verbal brainstorming, start with silent writing.

Why:

  • Introverts contribute equally
  • No groupthink
  • More diverse ideas
  • Faster

How: Everyone writes ideas on stickies for 5 minutes. Then share all at once.

Technique 2: Safety Check

Begin by asking: “How safe do you feel speaking honestly today (1–5)?”

If scores are low:

  • Acknowledge it
  • Use anonymous techniques
  • Address safety issues
  • Build trust over time

Why: Without safety, Retrospectives are theater.

Technique 3: Rotating Facilitators

Different team member facilitates each Sprint.

Benefits:

  • Distributes facilitation skills
  • Prevents facilitator burnout
  • Fresh perspectives
  • Increased ownership

Technique 4: Retrospective on Retrospectives

Every 3–4 Sprints, retrospect your Retrospectives.

Questions:

  • Are our Retrospectives effective?
  • Do we implement improvements?
  • What format works best?
  • How can we improve this event?

Technique 5: Working Agreements

Create explicit team agreements and revisit them.

Example agreements:

  • “We assume positive intent”
  • “We finish work before starting new work”
  • “We help teammates before starting new tasks”
  • “We respond to messages within 2 hours”

Review quarterly: Are we honoring these? Do they need updating?

Technique 6: Data-Driven Retrospectives

Bring actual data to discussions.

Examples:

  • Velocity trends
  • Bug counts
  • Cycle time
  • Sprint Goal achievement rate
  • Code review turnaround time

Why: Data replaces opinions with facts.

The Scrum Master’s Role in Retrospectives

Before:

  • Select appropriate format
  • Prepare materials
  • Create safe space
  • Review previous improvements

During:

  • Facilitate discussion
  • Ensure everyone participates
  • Keep time
  • Manage group dynamics
  • Remain neutral
  • Drive toward actionable outcomes

After:

  • Document improvements
  • Track implementation
  • Follow up throughout Sprint
  • Ensure accountability

Critical skills:

  • Active listening
  • Powerful questioning
  • Creating safety
  • Managing conflict
  • Staying neutral
  • Driving decisions

Common Retrospective Problems and Solutions

Problem 1: “Same Problems Every Sprint”

Why it happens:

  • Improvements aren’t implemented
  • Root causes not addressed
  • No accountability
  • Systemic issues beyond team control

Solutions:

  • Track improvement implementation like Sprint Backlog items
  • Use Five Whys to find root causes
  • Assign clear ownership
  • Escalate systemic impediments
  • Review progress in next Retrospective
  • Start smaller — achievable improvements build momentum

Problem 2: “Nobody Speaks Honestly”

Why it happens:

  • Low psychological safety
  • Fear of consequences
  • Blame culture
  • Managers attending

Solutions:

  • No managers/external stakeholders
  • Use anonymous techniques (digital tools, written feedback)
  • Scrum Master models vulnerability
  • Reinforce Prime Directive
  • Address retaliation immediately
  • Build safety over time through small wins

Problem 3: “It’s Just Complaining”

Why it happens:

  • No focus on solutions
  • Victim mentality
  • No facilitation structure
  • Learned helplessness

Solutions:

  • For every problem, require a proposed solution
  • Ask “What can we control?” Focus there
  • Use solution-focused questions: “What would it take to fix this?”
  • Celebrate small wins to build efficacy
  • Shift from “this sucks” to “how might we improve this?”

Problem 4: “Nothing Ever Changes”

Why it happens:

  • No follow-through
  • Improvements too big
  • No ownership
  • Team lacks authority

Solutions:

  • Limit to 1–2 improvements per Sprint
  • Make improvements small and specific
  • Assign owners explicitly
  • Check progress in Daily Scrum
  • Start next Retrospective by reviewing previous improvements
  • Escalate organizational impediments

Problem 5: “Same Format Every Time”

Why it happens:

  • Facilitator comfort zone
  • Lack of creativity
  • Team doesn’t request change

Solutions:

  • Rotate formats every 2–3 Sprints
  • Ask team what formats they want
  • Try new activities
  • Retrospect your Retrospectives
  • Make variety explicit: “Today we’re trying something different”

Problem 6: “Senior People Dominate”

Why it happens:

  • Hierarchy culture
  • Personality differences
  • Lack of facilitation

Solutions:

  • Silent writing first
  • Round-robin (everyone speaks)
  • Explicitly invite quiet voices: “Sarah, we haven’t heard from you”
  • Use talking stick or token
  • Private conversation with dominators
  • Structure prevents dominance

Special Retrospective Scenarios

First Retrospective (New Team)

Focus: Build foundation and safety.

Approach:

  • Simple format (Start/Stop/Continue)
  • More time on setting stage
  • Teach Retrospective purpose
  • Low-risk topics first
  • Emphasize Prime Directive
  • Small, achievable improvements

After a Failed Sprint

Focus: Learn without blame.

Approach:

  • Acknowledge emotions
  • Reinforce safety strongly
  • Focus on system, not individuals
  • What can we learn?
  • What will we do differently?
  • Find one success to celebrate

After a Great Sprint

Focus: Sustain success and raise the bar.

Approach:

  • Celebrate genuinely
  • Identify what made it great
  • How do we replicate this?
  • What’s the next level of performance?
  • Don’t skip Retrospective just because things went well

Conflict in the Team

Focus: Address issues constructively.

Approach:

  • Create extra safety
  • Use structured formats
  • Focus on behaviors, not personalities
  • Find common ground first
  • May need one-on-ones first
  • Scrum Master mediates if needed

Remote Retrospective

Challenges: Technology, engagement, reading body language.

Solutions:

  • Use digital collaboration tools (Miro, Mural, Retrium)
  • Cameras on
  • Longer timeboxes for writing
  • More frequent check-ins
  • Clear instructions
  • Use polls and reactions
  • Record for absent members

Tracking and Implementing Improvements

The Gap: Many teams identify improvements but never implement them.

Best Practices:

1. Limit Number One to three improvements maximum per Sprint.

2. Make Visible Add improvement items to Sprint Backlog or create separate board.

3. Assign Ownership Someone is responsible for driving implementation.

4. Check Progress Mention in Daily Scrum or set specific check-in times.

5. Review Next Retrospective Start by reviewing: Did we implement last Sprint’s improvements? What was the impact?

6. Celebrate Success When an improvement works, acknowledge it explicitly.

7. Adapt or Abandon If an improvement isn’t working, adapt it or drop it. Not everything will succeed.

Example tracking:

Improvement: “Pair program on all new features”

Owner: Developer A coordinates pairings

Tracking:

  • Week 1: Three stories paired, one solo
  • Week 2: All stories paired
  • Retrospective Review: Bug count down 40%. Continue practice.

Measuring Retrospective Effectiveness

Signs of Effective Retrospectives:

  • Improvements are implemented consistently
  • Team performance improves over time
  • Team looks forward to Retrospectives
  • Honest, constructive discussion happens
  • Different team members contribute
  • Actionable outcomes every Sprint

Signs of Ineffective Retrospectives:

  • Same problems surface repeatedly
  • Improvements aren’t implemented
  • Attendance drops or energy is low
  • Only superficial issues discussed
  • No real change results
  • Team dreads the event

What to measure:

  • Implementation rate of improvements
  • Sprint Goal achievement trend
  • Velocity stability and growth
  • Team happiness scores
  • Quality metrics (bugs, incidents)
  • Team’s own assessment of Retrospective value

Retrospective Anti-Patterns

Anti-Pattern 1: The Complaint Festival

What it looks like: Two hours of complaining. No solutions. No accountability. Just venting.

Why it’s wrong: Demoralizes team. Changes nothing.

Fix: For every problem, require proposed solution. Focus on what team can control.

Anti-Pattern 2: The Same Old Story

What it looks like: Same format. Same issues. Same (lack of) action. Every Sprint.

Why it’s wrong: Team loses faith in Retrospective value.

Fix: Vary formats. Review previous improvements. Make changes visible. Hold team accountable.

Anti-Pattern 3: The Skip

What it looks like: “We’re too busy this Sprint. Let’s skip Retrospective.”

Why it’s wrong: Improvement is never “too busy.” Skipping normalizes neglecting improvement.

Fix: Never skip. Shorten if necessary, but always hold it.

Anti-Pattern 4: The Manager Attendance

What it looks like: Leadership attends Retrospectives to “hear what’s happening.”

Why it’s wrong: Destroys psychological safety. Team won’t speak honestly.

Fix: Scrum Team only. No exceptions. Leaders get feedback through other channels.

Anti-Pattern 5: The Action-Free Zone

What it looks like: Great discussion. Lots of ideas. Zero commitment to action.

Why it’s wrong: Discussion without action is just talk.

Fix: Never end without at least one specific, assigned, trackable improvement.

Anti-Pattern 6: The Individual Blame

What it looks like: “The problem is that John never finishes his work on time.”

Why it’s wrong: Violates Prime Directive. Creates defensiveness. Damages relationships.

Fix: Focus on system. “Work isn’t finishing on time — what’s making it difficult?” Address individual performance issues privately.

Advanced Topics

Organizational Impediments

Challenge: Team identifies systemic issues beyond their control.

Approach:

  • Document clearly
  • Scrum Master escalates
  • Track progress
  • Keep team informed
  • Focus team on what they can control
  • Celebrate when organizational impediments are resolved

Sensitive Topics

Challenge: Issues involving specific individuals, conflicts, or personal matters.

Approach:

  • Create additional safety
  • Use anonymous techniques
  • May require one-on-one conversations first
  • Focus on behaviors and impacts, not personalities
  • Scrum Master may need to mediate
  • Consider external facilitator for particularly sensitive situations

Cross-Team Issues

Challenge: Problems involve multiple teams.

Approach:

  • Document in individual team Retrospectives
  • Organize cross-team Retrospective periodically
  • Scrum Masters coordinate
  • Focus on interfaces and dependencies
  • Create cross-team working agreements

Sample Retrospectives: Good vs. Bad

Bad Retrospective Example

3:00 PM — Meeting Room

Scrum Master: “Okay, let’s start the Retrospective. What went well this Sprint?”

Developer A: “Nothing really.”

Long silence

Scrum Master: “Come on, something must have gone well.”

Developer B: “I guess the deployment went smoothly.”

Scrum Master: “Great. What went wrong?”

Developer C: “The Product Owner kept changing requirements.”

Developer D: “Yeah, and we had too many meetings.”

Developer E: “And the code quality was terrible.”

Developer C: “That’s because John doesn’t write tests.”

John: “I don’t have time because I’m always fixing production bugs!”

Team argues for 10 minutes

Scrum Master: “Okay, let’s move on. What should we improve?”

Developer B: “Better communication.”

Developer D: “Yeah, and better code quality.”

Scrum Master: “Great. Let’s try to do that next Sprint. Anything else?”

Silence

Scrum Master: “Okay, we’re done.”

Meeting ends after 40 minutes. No specific improvements. No ownership. No real progress.

Good Retrospective Example

3:00 PM — Meeting Room

Scrum Master: “Welcome to our Sprint 16 Retrospective. Let’s start by reading the Prime Directive together…”

(Team reads Prime Directive aloud)

Scrum Master: “Check-in: One word to describe this Sprint for you.”

Team: “Productive” “Challenging” “Frustrating” “Learning” “Exhausting” “Good”

Scrum Master: “Thanks. I’m hearing mixed experiences. Let’s understand why. Today we’re doing a timeline exercise.”

(Scrum Master draws timeline on board)

Scrum Master: “Take 5 minutes. Add significant events, emotions, or learnings to the timeline silently.”

(Team adds sticky notes to timeline)

Scrum Master: “Let’s walk through this. What patterns do you see?”

Developer A: “There’s a cluster of frustration mid-Sprint when requirements changed.”

Developer B: “And a spike of positive emotion when we solved that production bug quickly.”

Scrum Master: “Good observations. Let’s dig into the requirement changes. What happened?”

Product Owner: “I learned from user testing mid-Sprint that our assumption was wrong. I changed the acceptance criteria.”

Developer C: “That threw off our plan. We had to redo work.”

Scrum Master: “What could have prevented this? Five whys — why did the requirement change mid-Sprint?”

Team discusses, concludes: User testing should happen during Refinement, not mid-Sprint.

Scrum Master: “So one improvement might be: move user testing earlier. Let’s keep going through the timeline…”

(Team continues discussing patterns)

Scrum Master: “Okay, we’ve identified several themes:

  1. Requirements changing mid-Sprint
  2. Production bugs interrupting work
  3. Code review delays
  4. Great team collaboration on critical problems

Let’s generate improvements. Five minutes of silent brainstorming.”

(Team writes improvement ideas)

Scrum Master: “Let’s share and group these.”

(Team shares 12 improvement ideas, groups into 4 themes)

Scrum Master: “We can’t do everything. Each person gets 3 dots — vote for your top priorities.”

(Team votes)

Top 3:

  1. Add user testing to Refinement process (8 dots)
  2. Establish 24-hour code review SLA (7 dots)
  3. Dedicate one developer to production support rotation (6 dots)

Scrum Master: “Let’s make these specific. For user testing in Refinement — what exactly will we do?”

Product Owner: “I’ll schedule 2–3 user interviews before each Refinement session. We’ll refine based on findings.”

Scrum Master: “Who owns this?”

Product Owner: “I will. Starting next week.”

Scrum Master: “How will we track it?”

Product Owner: “I’ll report in Daily Scrum when user testing is scheduled.”

(Team repeats for other two improvements with specific actions and ownership)

Scrum Master: “Before we close, let’s appreciate what went well. Who wants to recognize a teammate?”

Developer B: “I appreciate Developer A for pairing with me on that gnarly bug. Couldn’t have solved it alone.”

Developer A: “I appreciate the Product Owner for being so available this Sprint.”

Scrum Master: “Check-out: How confident are you (1–5) we’ll implement these improvements?”

Team: Averages 4.2 out of 5

Scrum Master: “Great. Let’s do this. See you in Planning tomorrow.”

Meeting ends after 1 hour 20 minutes. Three specific improvements. Clear ownership. Team energized.

Conclusion

The Sprint Retrospective is where teams evolve from good to great. It’s where continuous improvement isn’t just a slogan — it’s a practiced discipline.

Effective Retrospectives:

  • Create psychological safety for honest discussion
  • Surface problems early before they become crises
  • Generate specific, actionable improvements
  • Build team cohesion through shared reflection
  • Compound small improvements into major transformation
  • Make the team feel heard and empowered

The power of Retrospectives isn’t in the format or the activities. It’s in the commitment to pause, reflect, learn, and improve, every single Sprint without exception.

Great teams don’t stay great by accident. They continuously examine themselves, identify what’s not working, and fix it. Sprint by Sprint. Improvement by improvement. That’s the path from mediocrity to excellence.

Make Retrospective your team’s superpower. Create safety. Be honest. Focus on solutions. Take action. Follow through. Repeat.

The teams that master Retrospective master continuous improvement. And teams that master continuous improvement outperform everyone else.
