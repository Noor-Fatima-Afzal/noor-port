The Five Scrum Values: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage

Introduction

The Five Scrum Values: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage

Introduction

You can follow every Scrum rule perfectly, hold all the meetings, use all the artifacts, assign all the roles, and still fail miserably. How? Because Scrum is more than a process. It’s built on values that shape how people behave and work together.

Imagine a sports team where players follow the playbook but don’t trust each other, hide mistakes, give up when losing, and blame teammates when things go wrong. They might know the rules, but they’ll never win championships.

The same applies to Scrum teams. The framework provides structure, but values provide the foundation for success. Without the Five Scrum Values, Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage, teams become robotic, dysfunctional, and ineffective.

Let me show you how these values transform good teams into great ones.

Why Values Matter in Scrum

Values are the invisible force that determines how teams behave when nobody’s watching, how they respond to pressure, and how they treat each other during difficult times.

The Three Pillars (Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation) tell you what to do.

The Five Values tell you how to do it, with the right mindset and behaviors.

Think of it this way:

  • Without Courage, there’s no real Transparency
  • Without Openness, Inspection is shallow
  • Without Commitment, Adaptation never happens
  • Without Respect, the team falls apart
  • Without Focus, nothing meaningful gets done

Let’s explore each value and see how it shows up in daily work.

Value 1: Commitment

What is Commitment?

Commitment means dedicating yourself to achieving the team’s goals and supporting each other in that pursuit.

It’s NOT about: Working 80-hour weeks, saying “yes” to everything, or never changing plans.

It IS about: Doing your best work, honoring your promises to the team, and staying dedicated to the Sprint Goal even when challenges arise.

Why Commitment Matters

Without commitment, teams become collections of individuals pursuing personal agendas. With commitment, they become unified forces working toward shared success.

Commitment in Action

Daily Commitment You attend the Daily Scrum prepared and engaged. You don’t just show up, you actively participate and help teammates.

Sprint Commitment When the team selects work for a Sprint, everyone commits to doing their best to achieve the Sprint Goal. Not “I’ll try,” but “I’m committed.”

Quality Commitment You don’t cut corners to meet deadlines. You’re committed to delivering work that meets the Definition of Done.

Team Commitment When a teammate struggles, you help them. Their success is your success. Their failure is your failure.

Real-World Example

Without Commitment: Mid-Sprint, a developer realizes a feature is harder than expected. They don’t mention it, hoping to figure it out alone. By Sprint end, it’s incomplete. The team misses the Sprint Goal because one person wasn’t committed to asking for help.

With Commitment: The same developer mentions the difficulty in Daily Scrum. Another developer offers to pair program. They solve it together in half the time. The team achieves the Sprint Goal because everyone was committed to team success, not individual glory.

Building Commitment

Set Realistic Goals Teams can only commit to achievable goals. Overloading them destroys commitment.

Make Goals Meaningful People commit to work that matters. Connect Sprint Goals to real user value.

Remove Impediments Commitment wavers when obstacles block progress. Scrum Masters must clear the path.

Celebrate Collective Success Reward team achievements, not individual heroics. This reinforces commitment to shared goals.

Allow Safe Commitment Teams commit when they feel safe. If they’re punished for missing goals, they’ll stop committing honestly.

Value 2: Focus

What is Focus?

Focus means concentrating on the work of the Sprint and the goals of the Scrum Team.

In simple terms: Do one thing well rather than ten things poorly.

Why Focus Matters

Multitasking is a myth. Context switching destroys productivity. When teams try to do everything simultaneously, they complete nothing effectively.

Focus creates flow, flow creates quality, and quality creates value.

Focus in Action

Sprint Focus The team works only on items in the Sprint Backlog. They don’t get distracted by urgent requests, shiny new ideas, or management pet projects.

Daily Focus During Daily Scrum, the conversation focuses on progress toward the Sprint Goal, not random updates or technical debates.

Meeting Focus Sprint Planning focuses on planning. Retrospectives focus on improvement. Each event has a clear purpose and stays on topic.

Individual Focus Developers work on one task at a time until it’s done, rather than starting ten tasks and finishing none.

Real-World Example

Without Focus: A team commits to five user stories for the Sprint. Mid-Sprint, the CEO wants a quick report feature. The Product Owner adds it. A customer calls with an urgent bug. Two developers drop their work. By Sprint end, they have seven partially completed items and zero finished features.

With Focus: The same team commits to five user stories. The CEO requests a report. The Product Owner adds it to the Product Backlog for future consideration. The urgent bug is triaged — if it’s truly critical, the team cancels the Sprint and addresses it. Otherwise, it waits. The team finishes all five committed stories.

Types of Focus

Strategic Focus Working on the most valuable features first, not just the easiest or most interesting.

Tactical Focus Completing started work before starting new work. “Stop starting, start finishing.”

Team Focus Helping teammates complete their work before starting your next task. Swarm on blockers.

Meeting Focus Starting and ending meetings on time. Staying on agenda. Parking off-topic discussions.

Building Focus

Protect the Sprint The Sprint Backlog is sacred. New work doesn’t enter mid-Sprint without extraordinary justification.

Limit Work in Progress Each developer should work on one, maybe two items maximum at a time.

Time-Box Everything Set clear time limits for meetings and activities. This forces focus and prevents rambling.

Create Focus Time Establish “no meeting” blocks where developers can concentrate without interruption.

Visualize Work Use boards that show work in progress. Make it painfully obvious when too many items are “in progress.”

Value 3: Openness

What is Openness?

Openness means being transparent about your work, challenges, and progress. It means creating an environment where people share information freely.

In simple terms: No secrets, no hidden agendas, no pretending everything’s fine when it’s not.

Why Openness Matters

Problems hidden are problems multiplied. When people hide difficulties, small issues become catastrophes. When they share openly, the team can solve problems together quickly.

Openness is the lubricant that makes the Three Pillars work smoothly.

Openness in Action

Open About Challenges “I’m stuck on this technical problem” said openly in Daily Scrum, not hidden for days.

Open About Mistakes “I made an error that caused a bug” admitted immediately, not covered up or blamed on someone else.

Open About Capacity “I can’t take more work this Sprint” stated honestly, not accepted and then failed silently.

Open About Feedback “This feature doesn’t meet user needs” shared directly in Sprint Review, not sugarcoated or avoided.

Real-World Example

Without Openness: A developer discovers their code approach won’t scale to handle expected user load. They’re embarrassed to admit the mistake, so they work nights and weekends trying to fix it alone. Two weeks later, they’re burned out, the code still doesn’t work, and the release is delayed by a month.

With Openness: The same developer discovers the scaling issue. They openly share it in Daily Scrum: “My approach won’t scale. I need help redesigning this.” The team holds a quick design session. A senior developer suggests a better approach. They implement it together in three days.

Levels of Openness

Openness About Work Sharing what you’re working on, progress made, and plans for the day.

Openness About Problems Admitting when you’re blocked, stuck, or need help without fear of judgment.

Openness About Process Speaking honestly in Retrospectives about what’s working and what’s not.

Openness About Emotions Sharing when you’re frustrated, overwhelmed, or excited about the work.

Openness About Feedback Giving and receiving honest, constructive feedback without defensiveness.

Building Openness

Lead by Example Leaders and Scrum Masters must model openness by admitting their own mistakes and challenges.

Reward Honesty When someone shares bad news early, thank them. Make it clear that honesty is valued over looking good.

No Blame Culture Never punish people for being open about problems. Focus on solving issues, not finding scapegoats.

Ask Open Questions Instead of “Everything going fine?” ask “What challenges are you facing?”

Make Information Accessible Use visible boards, shared documents, and open communication channels. Information shouldn’t be locked away.

Value 4: Respect

What is Respect?

Respect means valuing people as capable, independent individuals. It means treating teammates, stakeholders, and users with dignity.

In simple terms: Treat others the way they want to be treated. Value their time, opinions, and contributions.

Why Respect Matters

Without respect, teams become toxic. People stop sharing ideas, avoid helping each other, and focus on self-preservation rather than team success.

With respect, teams become psychologically safe places where people do their best work.

Respect in Action

Respect for Expertise Trusting developers to make technical decisions. Trusting the Product Owner to prioritize. Not micromanaging.

Respect for Time Starting and ending meetings punctually. Coming prepared. Not wasting people’s time with unnecessary meetings.

Respect for Opinions Listening when others speak. Considering different perspectives. Not dismissing ideas without discussion.

Respect for Work-Life Balance Not expecting overtime. Not messaging teammates at midnight. Recognizing people have lives outside work.

Respect for Roles Understanding and honoring the boundaries of each Scrum role. Not overstepping or undermining.

Real-World Example

Without Respect: During Sprint Planning, a manager questions every estimate: “That should only take two hours, not two days.” Developers feel disrespected and distrusted. They stop giving honest estimates, instead inflating them or agreeing to impossible timelines to avoid conflict.

With Respect: The same manager asks, “Can you help me understand what makes this a two-day task?” The developers explain the technical complexity. The manager learns something new and trusts their expertise. Estimates become more accurate because developers feel respected and safe being honest.

Forms of Respect

Respect for Knowledge Recognizing that people closest to the work know it best. Deferring to their expertise.

Respect for Effort Acknowledging the hard work people do, even when results aren’t perfect.

Respect for Diversity Valuing different backgrounds, perspectives, and approaches as strengths, not obstacles.

Respect for Feedback Receiving criticism gracefully and giving it constructively.

Respect for Boundaries Understanding people’s limits and not pushing them beyond sustainable pace.

Building Respect

Listen Actively Put down your phone. Make eye contact. Actually hear what people are saying.

Acknowledge Contributions Say “thank you.” Recognize good work publicly. Show appreciation regularly.

Assume Positive Intent When someone makes a mistake, assume they were trying to do the right thing, not sabotaging.

Seek to Understand Before disagreeing, ask questions. Try to see the situation from their perspective.

Model Respect Treat everyone with respect, regardless of their role or seniority. Respect flows from example.

Value 5: Courage

What is Courage?

Courage means doing the right thing even when it’s difficult. It means tackling tough problems, having hard conversations, and admitting when you’re wrong.

In simple terms: Bravery to face uncomfortable truths and take necessary actions.

Why Courage Matters

Easy things don’t require courage. But in Scrum, teams constantly face difficult situations:

  • Admitting you’re behind schedule
  • Telling stakeholders a feature won’t work
  • Challenging a bad decision
  • Changing direction when you’re wrong
  • Having conflict conversations

Without courage, teams take the easy path — hiding problems, avoiding conflict, and delivering mediocre results.

Courage in Action

Courage to Say No “We can’t add this feature mid-Sprint” said to demanding stakeholders, protecting the Sprint Goal.

Courage to Admit Mistakes “My design was wrong. We need to redo this work” admitted openly, even though it’s embarrassing.

Courage to Ask for Help “I don’t understand this technology. Can someone teach me?” asked without shame.

Courage to Challenge “I disagree with this decision. Here’s why…” stated respectfully but firmly.

Courage to Change “This approach isn’t working. Let’s try something different” proposed even after investing significant effort.

Real-World Example

Without Courage: The team realizes their Sprint Goal is impossible to achieve by day 7 of a 10-day Sprint. Nobody wants to admit failure or disappoint stakeholders. They work overtime, cut corners on quality, and deliver a buggy, incomplete feature. Users are unhappy, and technical debt accumulates.

With Courage: The same team realizes the Sprint Goal is unreachable by day 7. They have the courage to call a meeting with the Product Owner: “We need to reduce scope or extend the Sprint. Here’s why and here are our options.” They make a tough decision together, deliver reduced but quality work, and learn for future Sprints.

Types of Courage

Moral Courage Standing up for what’s right, even when it’s unpopular or risky.

Intellectual Courage Admitting when you don’t know something or challenging accepted wisdom.

Emotional Courage Having difficult conversations, sharing feelings, and being vulnerable.

Professional Courage Pushing back on bad decisions, saying no to unreasonable demands, and protecting team health.

Building Courage

Create Psychological Safety People are only courageous when they feel safe. Build an environment where courage is rewarded, not punished.

Start Small Practice courage in low-stakes situations. Build the muscle gradually.

Support Each Other When someone shows courage, back them up. Don’t let them stand alone.

Share Stories Talk about times when courage led to better outcomes. Make it visible and celebrated.

Lead with Courage Leaders must demonstrate courage first. Teams follow their example.

How the Five Values Work Together

The values aren’t independent — they reinforce each other:

Courage enables Openness (being brave enough to share hard truths)

Openness builds Respect (being honest shows respect for others)

Respect strengthens Commitment (valuing each other deepens dedication)

Commitment enhances Focus (dedication to goals prevents distraction)

Focus requires Courage (saying no takes bravery)

And the cycle continues.

A Complete Example

The Situation: Mid-Sprint, a developer discovers the chosen technical approach won’t work. Starting over means missing the Sprint Goal.

Courage: The developer has the courage to admit the mistake immediately, not hide it.

Openness: They openly share the problem in Daily Scrum with full details.

Respect: The team respects the developer’s honesty and expertise. Nobody blames them.

Commitment: Everyone commits to solving this together, not leaving the developer alone.

Focus: The team focuses all effort on redesigning the solution, parking other work temporarily.

Result: They deliver a slightly reduced scope but with a solid technical foundation. The developer feels supported, not punished. The team grows stronger.

Common Challenges with Values

Challenge 1: Values as Posters

The Problem: Organizations print the values on posters but never actually practice them.

The Solution: Make values part of daily conversations. In Retrospectives, ask: “How did we live our values this Sprint?”

Challenge 2: Selective Values

The Problem: Teams embrace some values (like Focus) but ignore others (like Courage).

The Solution: All five values are necessary. Audit your team regularly to see which values are strong and which need attention.

Challenge 3: Cultural Conflicts

The Problem: Organizational culture contradicts Scrum values (e.g., blame culture vs. Openness).

The Solution: Start small. Create a safe bubble within your team. Demonstrate success. Gradually expand.

Challenge 4: Values Under Pressure

The Problem: Values disappear when deadlines loom or stakes are high.

The Solution: Values matter most when they’re hardest. Talk explicitly about maintaining values during pressure.

Practical Tips for Living the Values

Daily Practices

Morning:

  • Commit to being present in Daily Scrum
  • Focus on completing yesterday’s started work
  • Prepare to be open about challenges

During Work:

  • Respect teammates’ focus time — don’t interrupt needlessly
  • Have the courage to ask for help when stuck early
  • Stay focused on Sprint Backlog items

End of Day:

  • Commit to updating work status transparently
  • Show respect by not sending late-night messages
  • Be open about tomorrow’s potential challenges

In Meetings

Daily Scrum:

  • Courage: Share blockers honestly
  • Focus: Keep updates concise and relevant
  • Openness: Don’t hide problems

Sprint Planning:

  • Respect: Honor the timebox
  • Commitment: Only commit to achievable work
  • Focus: Plan for the Sprint Goal, nothing else

Sprint Review:

  • Openness: Show work as it is, not as you wish it were
  • Respect: Value stakeholder feedback
  • Courage: Admit what didn’t work

Retrospective:

  • Courage: Discuss real issues, not surface problems
  • Respect: Listen without defensiveness
  • Commitment: Actually implement improvements

Measuring Values

How do you know if your team is living the values?

Commitment Indicators:

  • Sprint Goals are achieved consistently
  • Team members help each other without being asked
  • People stay engaged even during difficult Sprints

Focus Indicators:

  • Low number of in-progress items
  • Few mid-Sprint scope changes
  • Meetings stay on topic and end on time

Openness Indicators:

  • Problems surface early, not late
  • No surprises at Sprint Review
  • People share mistakes freely

Respect Indicators:

  • Everyone’s voice is heard in meetings
  • No interrupting or talking over others
  • Work-life balance is maintained

Courage Indicators:

  • People challenge decisions when needed
  • Difficult topics get discussed, not avoided
  • The team says “no” when appropriate

Conclusion

The Five Scrum Values, Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage, transform Scrum from a mechanical process into a living, breathing way of working.

Commitment unites the team toward common goals. Focus ensures energy goes to what matters most. Openness creates transparency and trust. Respect builds psychological safety and collaboration. Courage enables teams to tackle hard problems and grow.

You can’t buy these values. You can’t install them. You must practice them daily, in small moments and big decisions, until they become who you are as a team.

The practices of Scrum provide the structure. The values provide the soul.

Without values, you’re just going through motions. With values, you’re building something extraordinary.
